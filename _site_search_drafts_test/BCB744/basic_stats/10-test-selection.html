10. Choosing the Right Test

A Practical Decision Guide for Core Inferential Questions

Author

A. J. Smit

1 Introduction

Many students first encounter statistics as a menu of named tests. That is not the best way to choose a method. In practice, test selection begins with the biological question, the structure of the data, and the design of the study.

By the time you reach this chapter, you have already met the main inferential tools used in the first part of the course:

  • one-sample, two-sample, and paired t-tests;
  • ANOVA and its common extensions;
  • correlation and association;
  • proportion tests;
  • their most common non-parametric alternatives.

This chapter helps you choose among the tests you already know.

2 Key Concepts

A rule of thumb to follow is to choose a method by question, data structure, and design together.

  • Test selection starts with the question, not the software function.
  • Variable type is important because some methods are only appropriate for continuous data, others for counts, categories, or binary outcomes.
  • Design is also important because independence, pairing, and repeated measurement change the appropriate analysis.
  • Assumptions matter because they determine whether a parametric method is reasonable or whether an alternative is needed.
  • Methods connect because the logic of one-sample tests, group comparisons, association, and modelling forms a sequence rather than a collection of unrelated techniques.

3 The Decision Steps

To find the right test, work through the following questions in order:

  1. What is the biological question?
  2. What is the response variable, and what kind of data is it?
  3. How many groups, variables, or predictors are involved?
  4. Are the observations independent, paired, or repeated?
  5. Are the assumptions of the preferred method plausible?

If you can answer those five questions clearly, the correct family of methods is usually obvious.

5 Choosing by Question Type

5.1 Is the question about one mean or one proportion?

If you have a single sample and want to compare it against a known or hypothesised value:

  • use a one-sample t-test for a continuous response when the assumptions are plausible;
  • use a one-sample Wilcoxon signed-rank test when the one-sample t-test assumptions fail badly;
  • use a one-sample proportion test when the response is binary and the question concerns a probability of success.

Typical example questions include:

  • Is the mean height of a sample of Protea plants different from a known population mean?
  • Is the proportion of African penguins foraging in one colony different from an established reference proportion?

This is the approach developed in Chapter 7: t-Tests.

5.2 Is the question about two groups?

If you want to compare two groups:

  • use an independent two-sample t-test when the groups are independent and the response is continuous;
  • use a paired t-test when the same individuals are measured twice or when observations are deliberately matched;
  • use a Wilcoxon rank-sum test as a non-parametric alternative for two independent groups;
  • use a Wilcoxon signed-rank test for non-parametric paired data;
  • use a two-sample proportion test when the response is binary and the question concerns two proportions.

Typical example questions include:

  • Is the average number of leopard cubs per female different between the Overberg and Cederberg regions?
  • Did piglet body mass change between the start and end of a feeding period?
  • Do two beaches differ in the proportion of hatchling sea turtles that reach the ocean?

Again, this thinking is developed Chapter 7: t-Tests.

5.3 Is the question about three or more groups?

If the response is continuous and the predictor is a categorical factor with three or more levels:

  • use a one-way ANOVA when the assumptions are plausible;
  • use a factorial ANOVA when more than one categorical factor is involved;
  • use a Kruskal-Wallis test when a one-way ANOVA is not appropriate and a rank-based alternative is acceptable.

Typical example questions include:

  • Does grasshopper chirp rate differ among the four seasons?
  • Do mean responses differ among several treatments, habitats, or vegetation types?

See Chapter 8: ANOVA for further information about ANOVAs.

5.4 Is the question about association between two variables?

If the question is whether two variables vary together:

  • use Pearson correlation for approximately linear association among continuous variables;
  • use Spearman or Kendall correlation when rank-based association is more appropriate;
  • use regression instead if the real question is one of response and predictor rather than simple association.

Typical example questions include:

  • Do kelp stipe length and frond length co-vary across individuals?
  • Do two ranked biological traits increase together even when a linear model is not the right first description?

These methods are explored in Chapter 9: Correlation and Association.

6 Choosing by Data Structure

Another way to choose a method is to start with the data structure.

6.1 Continuous response with a categorical predictor

This is the basic comparison-of-means method:

  • one group against a reference value → one-sample t-test;
  • two groups → two-sample t-test;
  • paired measurements → paired t-test;
  • three or more groups → ANOVA.

6.2 Continuous response with a continuous explanatory variable

Here the distinction between association and modelling becomes important:

  • use correlation when the goal is to quantify association;
  • use regression when the goal is to model a response.

Typical example questions include:

  • How does bill length change with body mass in Adelie penguins?
  • Does algal abundance change with nutrient concentration once other predictors are considered?

6.3 Binary response

When the response is, for example, a success/failure outcome summarised as a proportion:

  • use proportion tests for one-sample or two-sample proportion questions;
  • go later to generalised linear models when the structure becomes more complicated.

Typical example questions include:

  • Does settlement success change with habitat or salinity?
  • Is survival probability better modelled as a function of treatment, size, or site?

6.4 Count response

Counts such as numbers of offspring, parasites, seeds, or individuals per quadrat usually do not belong in the same family as mean-comparison tests.

  • use generalised linear models with Poisson or negative-binomial logic when the response is a count;
  • consider overdispersion explicitly rather than forcing count data into a normal model.

Typical example questions include:

  • How does parasite load change with host size?
  • Does quadrat-level abundance depend on reef, depth, or season?

6.5 Repeated, clustered, or hierarchical data

If observations are repeated on the same individual, nested within sites, repeated within a time stratum, then the basic independent-sample tests are no longer enough.

  • move to mixed-effects or hierarchical models when repeated or nested structure matters;
  • treat this as a design issue (i.e., it is implicit in experimental design) first, not as a software fix added at the end.

Typical example questions include:

  • Do repeated chlorophyll-a measurements differ through time once bay or year is accounted for?
  • Does a treatment effect persist after recognising sites as random sampling units?

7 Extending Beyond the Staple Tests

The first half of this module focuses on the staple or core inferential statistical tools because they teach the reasoning behind matching a question to the data. But the full statistical toolbox is broader than the introductory named tests.

The important extensions are:

  • Simple linear regression when the response is continuous and the question is about one predictor and one response.
  • Multiple linear regression when the response is continuous and several predictors are needed together.
  • Generalised linear models (GLMs) when the response is binary, proportional, or count-based.
  • Generalised additive models (GAMs) when the response is clearly curved and a smooth data-driven mean structure is needed.
  • Nonlinear regression when the biology suggests a specific mechanistic curve.
  • Generalised nonlinear models (GNLM-style extensions) when the response is both non-normal and nonlinear; these are advanced extensions rather than basic introductory methods, but they belong in the broader conceptual landscape.
  • Mixed and hierarchical models when observations are repeated, clustered, or nested.

Those methods are not random extras. They are what the introductory tests grow into once the question becomes richer, the data structure becomes more realistic, or the response no longer fits the simple Gaussian setting.

8 Assumptions and Alternatives

Parametric methods are preferred because they use more information from the data and are usually more powerful when their assumptions are reasonable. However, this does not mean that you must force a parametric method onto unsuitable data.

A practical guide is:

  1. choose the method that matches the question and design;
  2. check whether its assumptions are plausible;
  3. if necessary, consider a transformation, a rank-based alternative, or a more suitable model.

For the material covered so far, the most important alternatives are:

  • one-sample or paired t-test → wilcox.test();
  • two-sample t-test → wilcox.test();
  • one-way ANOVA → kruskal.test();
  • Pearson correlation → Spearman or Kendall correlation.

Assumption checking itself is discussed in Chapter 6.

9 Common Mistakes in Test Selection

These are the most common reasons students choose the wrong method:

  • choosing a test because the function name is familiar rather than because the question fits;
  • ignoring whether the observations are paired or independent;
  • using correlation when the real aim is regression;
  • using many pairwise t-tests instead of ANOVA;
  • switching to a non-parametric test without first understanding which assumption has failed;
  • treating a binary response as if it were continuous.

11 Summary

Choose a method in this order:

  1. Start with the biological question.
  2. Identify the response type and explanatory structure.
  3. Decide whether observations are independent, paired, or repeated.
  4. Match the problem to the correct method family.
  5. Check assumptions and use alternatives only for a clear reason.
  6. Move to the richer model family when the response structure or design requires it.

If you follow this sequence, you will usually arrive at the right method family without having to memorise statistics as a disconnected list.

6. Move to the richer model family when the response structure or design requires it.

If you follow this sequence, you will usually arrive at the right method family without having to memorise statistics as a disconnected list.
